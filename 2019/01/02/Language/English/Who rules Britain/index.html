Who rules Britain

Jan 2, 2019 | English

Britain is a democracy(/dɪ’mɒkrəsɪ/民主，民权运动), which means we the people decide how the county is run. But instead of squeezing(压榨，挤压) 64 million people into the house of commons, we have 650 people representing all of us. How are they choosed? The country has been carefully divided into 650 constituencies(constituency/kən’stɪtjʊənsɪ/选民，选区). Each one containing roughly/‘rʌflɪ/ 70,000 voters. Every 5 years there’s a general selection. We put an “X” next to the person we like best, then the person with the most voters becomes our constituency’s Member of Parliament(/‘pɑːləm(ə)nt/议会，国会 ) or “MP”.

The queen politely ask one of MPs to be her Prime Minister(首相). Hello, your majesty/‘mædʒɪstɪ/.
The Prime Minister’s job is to decide who does what, swap them around every-so-often.
But the govenment can’t do whatever it likes. It has to be kept in check and challenged on every decision it makes. That’s mainly the job of the party that came second in the General Election. They’re called the “opposition”.
